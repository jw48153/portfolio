Impact of the Affordable Care Act on Coverage Rates (2010-2015)

üìä Project Overview

This comprehensive policy analysis evaluated state-level health insurance coverage data to assess the Affordable Care Act's effectiveness in reducing uninsured rates. By comparing 32 Medicaid expansion states against 19 non-expansion states, this project quantifies the direct impact of state policy decisions on healthcare access and coverage equity.

Finding #2: Non-Expansion States Relied More on Marketplace Coverage

Without Medicaid expansion, states needed 89% more marketplace enrollment with higher subsidies.

  • Non-expansion marketplace enrollment: 308,589 per state
  • Expansion marketplace enrollment: 163,067 per state (47% lower)
  • Non-expansion avg subsidy: $306.26/month (7% higher)
  • Despite higher marketplace usage, worse coverage achieved (11% vs 7% uninsured)

Insight: Marketplace subsidies alone cannot replace Medicaid expansion's effectiveness. Non-expansion states required more federal subsidy dollars to achieve inferior coverage outcomes.

Finding #3: Geographic Variation Reveals Implementation Disparities

State-by-state analysis shows significant variation even within policy categories.

  • Top performers achieved improvements exceeding national averages
  • Bottom performers showed minimal gains despite ACA availability
  • Implementation quality mattered as much as policy choice
  • Geographic health equity gaps persisted in non-expansion regions

Insight: Federal policy success depends on state-level execution and political will, creating geographic disparities in healthcare access.

üí° Policy Implications

1. Medicaid Expansion Proves Superior Strategy

Evidence supports expansion as the most effective coverage mechanism, achieving 20% better improvement while enrolling 3x more individuals at lower per-person costs than marketplace subsidies.

2. Enhanced Marketplace Support Needed

Non-expansion states required 89% more marketplace enrollment and higher subsidies, suggesting need for improved outreach and streamlined enrollment processes to maximize coverage.

3. Address Geographic Health Equity

Persistent state-to-state variation creates unequal healthcare access. Federal incentives for late-adopting states and coverage equity benchmarks could reduce geographic disparities.

Expected Outcome

If all non-expansion states adopted Medicaid expansion, national uninsured rates could decrease an additional 4 percentage points, covering millions more Americans while reducing federal subsidy costs through more efficient coverage mechanisms.
